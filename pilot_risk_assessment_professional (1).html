IMSAFE Checklist

IMSAFE is a systematic pilot self-assessment tool for evaluating fitness to fly.

Illness

Any symptom that could impair judgment or physical performance is a red flag. Fever, congestion, dizziness, or other conditions that worsen under flight stress should elevate your risk assessment immediately. High illness severity should trigger a no-go decision.

Medication

Every medication—prescription and over-the-counter—should be checked against FAA guidance. Side effects like drowsiness, blurred vision, or impaired coordination can degrade flight performance. Consult the Drug Aeronautical Information Manual (Drug AIM) before flying.

Stress

High stress drains cognitive bandwidth and leads to poor decision-making. Work deadlines, family conflict, or financial concerns can preoccupy you during flight. In GRM cost-benefit analysis, high stress multiplies other risks—high stress + marginal weather = unacceptable combined risk.

Alcohol

FAA mandates a minimum 8 hours between your last drink and flight. Residual impairment may persist beyond 8 hours, especially after heavy consumption. Apply personal minimums beyond regulatory minimums. When in doubt, treat alcohol risk as high.

Fatigue

Fatigue undermines every flight phase. Check your sleep over the past 24 hours (not just last night), quality of rest, and recent duty periods. If below your personal standard, plan to delay or cancel. Revisit fatigue at top-of-descent to catch "just one more leg" syndrome.

Emotion

Emotional volatility—anger, grief, excitement—hijacks rational decision-making. Recent conflicts or traumas can distract you. Externalize your emotional state aloud before flight and re-enter the DECIDE loop if emotions spike.

PAVE Framework

PAVE is a four-element checklist covering all operational aspects of flight planning and execution.

Pilot

Assess your readiness: flight hours, recency, currency in the aircraft type, familiarity with avionics and emergency procedures, and personal minimums. Confirm you're prepared to manage the workload of your planned operation.

Aircraft

Verify airworthiness documents, conduct thorough preflight inspection (fuel, oil, surfaces, tires), calculate weight and balance, and review POH performance charts for all flight phases. Deferred maintenance items (MEL) must be evaluated for your specific flight plan.

enVironment

Obtain full weather briefing including METARs, TAFs, winds aloft, AIRMETs, and SIGMETs. Check NOTAMs and TFRs along your route. Evaluate terrain and obstacle clearance. Plan alternates and diversion routes if conditions deteriorate.

External Pressures

Identify passenger expectations, schedule demands, organizational pressure, and personal goals that might tempt unsafe decisions. Establish a no-guilt go/no-go point before engine start and commit to contingency planning with adequate margin.

DECIDE Process

The DECIDE model is a six-step in-flight decision-making cycle that keeps you ahead of emerging hazards.

1. Detect

Maintain continuous, methodical vigilance. Scan instruments every 20–30 seconds and note anomalies. Watch for weather buildups, ATC frequency changes, system alerts, rising workload, or changes in cabin conditions. Use PAVE mentally at level-off and cruise.

2. Evaluate

Quickly assess severity and likelihood to gauge the threat to your safety margins. Rate severity: "how big an upset could this be?" Rate likelihood: "how probable is that outcome?" Translate into low, medium, or high risk.

3. Consider

Brainstorm at least three workable courses of action. Don't lock onto the first fix. Write options on your scratchpad. Engage your crew or passengers for input. For weather: change altitude, deviate laterally, request alternate vector.

4. Integrate

Merge chosen mitigations against aircraft limits, fuel requirements, regulations, and passenger needs. Compute new headings, distances, fuel burn, and ETAs if diverting. Amend your flight plan in the GPS and update your mental checklist.

5. Decide

Select one best plan and commit to it without hesitation. Make a strong verbal call-out: "I'm diverting to MXY. Heading 180, squawk 1200, ETE 12 min." Brief passengers and ATC immediately.

6. Execute & Reassess

Carry out the decision with precise control inputs and checklist discipline. Monitor outcomes. After diversion, re-scan PAVE in 5 minutes. If a new hazard emerges, restart DECIDE from Detect. This is a continuous loop, not a one-time decision.

Risk Scoring

This dashboard uses a weighted algorithm to convert your inputs into a quantified risk score (0–100%).

IMSAFE Score: Illness, Medication, and Emotion carry baseline weights. Fatigue carries 1.5× weight. Alcohol carries 1.8× weight because it is a critical safety factor. Stress acts as a multiplier (+0–30%) on all other IMSAFE factors.

PAVE Score: Pilot experience and currency contribute equally. Aircraft status carries 1.3× weight. Environment (weather, winds, terrain) carries 1.6× weight due to dynamic in-flight hazards. External Pressures carry baseline weight.

Total Risk: 45% IMSAFE + 55% PAVE, weighted toward operational factors.

Thresholds: 0–35% = GO, 35–60% = CAUTION, 60–100% = NO-GO

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is this tool approved by the FAA?

A: This dashboard implements frameworks endorsed by FAASTeam (IMSAFE, PAVE, DECIDE from the August 14 ADM/GRM seminar). It is a decision support tool, not an official FAA approval. The pilot in command is solely responsible for all go/no-go decisions.

Q: Where is my data stored?

A: All assessments are stored locally in your browser using browser localStorage. No data is transmitted to external servers. Your privacy is protected—history is stored only on your device.

Q: Can I use this on my tablet or phone?

A: Yes! The dashboard is fully responsive. Perfect for preflight briefing on any device.

Q: What if I get a "CAUTION" or "NO-GO" score?

A: Carefully review the flagged risk factors. Consider consulting a CFI, ATCO, or your chief pilot. Implement mitigations (adjust fuel planning, delay to rest, coordinate weather updates). If critical items persist, delay or cancel the flight.

Q: How often should I run this assessment?

A: Ideally as part of your standard preflight briefing. You can also re-assess mid-flight at top-of-descent or if conditions change unexpectedly.

  • PAVE: Operational risk factor evaluation
  • DECIDE: In-flight decision-making process

Together, these frameworks empower pilots to systematically identify hazards, assess their impact, and make disciplined go/no-go decisions grounded in evidence.

Risk-Based Approach

The scoring algorithm reflects real aviation safety principles:

  • Fatigue and alcohol weighted as critical factors
  • Stress acts as a multiplier on other risks
  • Weather and operational factors heavily weighted
  • Weighted toward operational readiness (55% vs 45%)

The result is a quantified risk score that guides your decision, not a binary pass/fail. Use it as a complement to your judgment, experience, and conservative personal minimums.
